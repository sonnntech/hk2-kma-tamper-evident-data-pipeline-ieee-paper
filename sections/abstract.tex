\begin{abstract}
Modern data pipelines process data through multiple stages, such as ingestion, transformation, storage, and analytical serving. After a pipeline finishes, however, many platforms do not provide an independent mechanism to verify whether the persisted data still matches the state produced during execution. Audit logs, application-level checksums, and monitoring dashboards are useful, but they often remain within the same trust boundary as the data or only observe runtime status. This paper presents a quasi-experimental evaluation of a lightweight hash-linked tamper-evident ledger for data pipeline integrity verification. The proposed mechanism records stage-level evidence, including record counts, schema hashes, batch hashes, block hashes, previous-hash links, and lineage events. A verification engine recomputes the evidence after execution and reports integrity violations. The study compares clean and tampered pipeline conditions, evaluates traceability to the first broken block and related lineage event, and estimates runtime overhead across different record counts. Experimental observations on a Databricks-based prototype show that all predefined tamper scenarios were detected within the tested scope, while the clean condition produced no false positive in the observed dashboard export. The overhead increased with record count, but the results remain bounded by cloud compute variability and the limited number of benchmark repetitions. The contribution of this work is not a full blockchain platform, but a controlled evaluation of a hash-linked tamper-evident mechanism for data integrity assurance in data pipelines.
\end{abstract}
